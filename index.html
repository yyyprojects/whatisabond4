A Tour of the U.S. Bond Market

U.S. Treasury Securities

Issued by the U.S. federal government to fund its operations. They are considered the safest investments in the world because they are backed by the "full faith and credit" of the U.S. government.

Type Maturity How Interest is Paid
Treasury Bills (T-Bills) 1 year or less Sold at a discount, you receive face value at maturity. No regular payments.
Treasury Notes (T-Notes) 2 to 10 years Pays interest every 6 months.
Treasury Bonds (T-Bonds) 20 or 30 years Pays interest every 6 months. Typically the highest rates of the three.

Municipal Bonds ("Munis")

Issued by state and local governments to fund public projects like schools, highways, and parks. Their main attraction is a significant tax advantage: interest is often exempt from federal income tax, and sometimes state and local taxes too.

Corporate Bonds

Issued by companies to raise money for things like building new factories or funding research. They offer higher interest rates than government bonds to compensate investors for taking on more risk, as repayment depends on the company's financial health.

Understanding the Key Risks

1. Interest Rate Risk (The See-Saw Effect)

This is the most common risk. It's an inverse relationship: when market interest rates go up, the price of your existing, lower-paying bond goes down. Why? Because new bonds are now more attractive. This risk mainly matters if you sell your bond before it matures.

2. Inflation Risk

This is the risk that the fixed interest payments you receive won't keep up with the rising cost of living. If your bond pays 3% interest but inflation is 4%, your money's purchasing power is actually shrinking.

3. Credit Risk (or Default Risk)

This is the straightforward risk that the issuer can't pay you back. It's the danger that the company or government will default on its loan. This risk is lowest for U.S. Treasuries and highest for lower-rated corporate "junk" bonds.

The Bottom Line

Bonds are a fundamental building block for a stable financial future. By understanding how they work, you can use them to generate income, protect your savings, and build a more resilient portfolio.
